A flower that speaks every language — passion, devotion, and eternal beauty.

No flower in human history has been written about, painted, sculpted, and sung about more than the rose. It is the flower of Shakespeare's sonnets, the symbol of war between English royal houses, the emblem of love in every culture that has ever known it — and with good reason. The rose is extraordinary.

An Ancient Flower with a Storied Past

Roses have existed for at least 35 million years, according to fossil evidence found in Colorado and Oregon. But their relationship with humanity is younger — and no less dramatic. The ancient Egyptians cultivated roses in sacred gardens, using petals in burial rites and cosmetics. Cleopatra, legend has it, filled her meeting chambers knee-deep in rose petals to greet Mark Antony.

In ancient Rome, roses were the flower of Venus, goddess of love, and were so prized that rose water was used as currency. The Romans built extravagant 'rosaria' — rose gardens — and scattered petals at banquets and public ceremonies. Their obsession with the rose was so intense that it nearly destabilized the wheat supply, as farmland was converted to rose cultivation.

During the Middle Ages, the rose became the symbol of the Virgin Mary — the 'Rose Without Thorns.' The Wars of the Roses (1455–1487) saw the houses of Lancaster (red rose) and York (white rose) tear England apart, only to be reconciled under the Tudor rose — a red and white union still on England's heraldic crest today.

The Language of Rose Colors

Perhaps no other flower communicates as precisely through color. The Victorian art of 'floriography' — sending messages through flowers — elevated the rose to a sophisticated communication tool. Each color speaks a different sentiment:

Red Rose — Passionate Love

The universal declaration of deep, romantic love. A single red rose says more than a thousand words ever could.

Pink Rose — Gratitude & Admiration

Soft pink expresses gentle affection and appreciation. Perfect for mothers, mentors, and dear friends.

Purple Rose — Enchantment

Purple roses signal mystery, majesty, and love at first sight — a flower for someone who takes your breath away.

White Rose — Purity & New Beginnings

The flower of weddings and fresh starts — white roses symbolize purity, innocence, and a hopeful future.

Maroon Rose — Unconscious Beauty

Deep maroon roses say 'you are beautiful without knowing it' — an expression of humble, profound admiration.

The Rose in Modern Floral Design

Today, the rose dominates the global cut-flower industry, accounting for a third of all flower sales worldwide. Colombia, Kenya, and Ecuador are the world's largest exporters, shipping billions of stems annually. In Indonesia, roses are grown in the cool highlands of Berastagi (North Sumatra) and Lembang (West Java), where the mountain climate produces particularly vibrant blooms.

In floral design, roses are the ultimate versatile flower — they anchor bouquets, fill arrangements, cascade in wedding arches, and stand beautifully alone. Their layered petals create a visual depth that few other flowers can match.

Garden Rose

Full, lush heads with densely packed petals. The romantic choice for weddings and opulent arrangements.

Spray Rose

Multiple small blooms per stem, perfect for adding texture and volume to bouquets and table arrangements.

Hybrid Tea Rose

The classic long-stem rose — high-centered, elegant, and the definitive gift rose since the early 20th century.

David Austin Rose

English garden roses celebrated for their cupped, old-fashioned form and intensely fragrant, swirling petals.

Roses in Indonesian Tradition

In Indonesian culture, roses hold deep social and ceremonial significance. They are the centerpiece flower at wedding receptions (known as pesta pernikahan), used in everything from table arrangements to ceremonial garlands. At graduation ceremonies, a bouquet of roses — especially the classic red or soft pink — is the most common gift given by proud families to their graduates.

Roses also appear prominently in selamatan (thanksgiving gatherings) and haul (Islamic memorial ceremonies), where their fragrance and beauty are offered as a form of respect and prayer.
